# Heuristic Conflicts and Resolution

Design heuristics frequently conflict. A decision that improves one dimension may degrade another. Expert design is largely the art of navigating these conflicts.

---

## Common Conflicts

### 1. Hierarchy vs. Minimalism

**The tension:** Hierarchy requires visual treatments to create emphasis. Minimalism requires reducing visual treatments to the essential.

**Resolution:** Minimalism means few visual treatments, not no visual treatments. Choose a small number of hierarchy signals and apply them consistently: one heading size, one body weight, one accent color. Create maximum hierarchy with minimum means.

**Decision rule:** If the reader cannot identify the most important element in under 2 seconds, hierarchy wins. If the reader is overwhelmed by visual noise, minimalism wins.

### 2. Completeness vs. Whitespace

**The tension:** Complete pages feel resolved when full. But filling every pixel destroys whitespace, which provides cognitive rest and signals relationships.

**Resolution:** Completeness comes from structure, not density. A page with 60% content and 40% whitespace can feel complete if the content is well-organized, whitespace is intentional, and the page has clear beginning and end.

**Decision rule:** If the page feels abandoned, completeness wins. If the page feels cramped, whitespace wins.

### 3. Consistency vs. Emphasis

**The tension:** Consistency means treating the same content the same way. Emphasis means breaking consistency to draw attention.

**Resolution:** Consistency is the default. Emphasis is the exception. Establish a consistent visual language first, break it deliberately and sparingly, and the break must be significant enough to register.

**Decision rule:** If the reader has been trained to expect a pattern, breaking it is expensive. Only break it when the emphasis justifies the cognitive cost of surprise.

### 4. Density vs. Rest

**The tension:** Dense information packs more into less space, which is efficient for reference. But density without rest is exhausting.

**Resolution:** Alternate dense and sparse sections. Use visual breathing room between dense blocks. Provide scan anchors (headings, whitespace, visual markers) so the reader can pause and re-orient.

**Decision rule:** If the reader needs to reference, not read linearly, density wins. If the reader needs sustained attention, rest wins.

### 5. Information Architecture vs. Visual Simplicity

**The tension:** Complex information may require complex structure (many categories, subcategories, cross-references). Visual simplicity demands reducing apparent complexity.

**Resolution:** Use progressive disclosure. Present the simple structure first, reveal complexity on demand. The surface should be simple; the depth should be rich.

**Decision rule:** If the reader is seeing everything at once, simplify. If the reader is trying to drill down, reveal complexity.

### 6. Convention vs. Differentiation

**The tension:** Conventions (standard layouts, common patterns, expected positions) reduce cognitive load because the reader already knows how they work. Differentiation makes your design distinctive and memorable.

**Resolution:** Follow conventions for structure and interaction. Differentiate through content, typography, and subtle visual treatment. Do not reinvent navigation, layout patterns, or information hierarchy for the sake of novelty.

**Decision rule:** If the reader needs to figure out how to use the design, convention wins. If the reader needs to remember the design, differentiation wins.

---

## Resolution Framework

When heuristics conflict:

1. **Identify the competing heuristics.** Name both sides clearly.
2. **Determine which serves the reader better in this context.** Not which is aesthetically preferred, but which reduces cognitive load more.
3. **Choose a primary and secondary.** One heuristic takes priority. The other is accommodated as much as possible without compromising the primary.
4. **Document the tradeoff.** In design rationale, name what was prioritized and what was sacrificed.
5. **Test the decision.** If possible, validate with actual readers. If not, apply the decision rules above.

---

## The Meta-Heuristic

When in doubt, ask: **Does this make the information easier to understand, or just easier to look at?**

If it makes information easier to understand, it is design.
If it only makes it easier to look at, it is decoration.
