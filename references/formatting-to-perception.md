# Formatting to Perception Translation Guide

A dictionary that converts common formatting instructions into their perceptual intent.

When given a formatting instruction, translate it to the perceptual goal first. Then execute the perceptual goal, not the literal instruction.

---

## Spacing Instructions

### "Add more white space"

**Perceptual intent:** The visual distance between content does not reflect the conceptual relationship between ideas. Increase spacing between unrelated sections to signal they are separate. Decrease spacing between related items to signal they belong together.

**How to execute:** Evaluate the conceptual relationships in the content. Increase inter-section spacing to 2-3x intra-section spacing. Ensure spacing is proportional to conceptual distance.

### "Reduce the white space"

**Perceptual intent:** The visual distance between content is too large for the conceptual relationship. Related items appear disconnected. The reader must work harder to connect ideas.

**How to execute:** Identify which elements are conceptually related. Reduce spacing between them. Keep spacing between unrelated sections large.

### "Space things out more evenly"

**Perceptual intent:** The spacing rhythm is inconsistent. The reader cannot form expectations about where the next element will appear. This creates cognitive friction.

**How to execute:** Establish a consistent spacing scale. Apply it proportionally. Intra-section spacing should be consistent. Inter-section spacing should be consistent at each level.

## Hierarchy Instructions

### "Make the headings bigger"

**Perceptual intent:** The visual hierarchy is insufficient. The reader cannot scan the page and identify the information structure. Headings do not stand out enough from body text.

**How to execute:** Increase the contrast between heading and body text. This may mean larger headings, but it could also mean heavier weight, more whitespace above/below, or a different color. The goal is scanability, not just size.

### "Add more headings"

**Perceptual intent:** The information structure is not visible. The reader must read paragraphs to find what they need. The content needs scan anchors.

**How to execute:** Identify the natural section boundaries. Add headings at those boundaries. Ensure headings tell the story (a reader should understand the structure from headings alone).

### "Break up the paragraph"

**Perceptual intent:** The visual chunk is too large for working memory. The reader is overwhelmed by the density. The information needs to be divided into manageable visual units.

**How to execute:** Identify the natural topic shifts within the paragraph. Create visual breaks at those points. This may mean actual paragraph breaks, sub-headings, lists, or visual separators.

### "Make this stand out more"

**Perceptual intent:** This element has insufficient emphasis relative to its importance. The reader's attention is not being directed to the right place.

**How to execute:** Increase the visual contrast between this element and its surroundings. Use size, weight, color, whitespace, or position. Choose the treatment that is most consistent with the existing visual language.

## Typography Instructions

### "Use a nicer font"

**Perceptual intent:** The current typeface is not communicating the right tone, or it is not supporting readability. The typography is working against comprehension.

**How to execute:** Evaluate what the typeface communicates. Choose a typeface that matches the content's tone (authoritative, friendly, technical, elegant). Ensure readability at the intended size and context.

### "Make the text bigger"

**Perceptual intent:** The text is not readable at its current size. Either the physical size is too small for the context, or the visual hierarchy does not distinguish this text from surrounding text.

**How to execute:** Evaluate readability in context. Increase size if the text is genuinely hard to read. If the text is readable but not prominent, the issue may be hierarchy, not size.

### "Use more fonts"

**Perceptual intent:** The current visual language lacks the variety needed to communicate different types of information. The reader cannot visually distinguish between categories.

**How to execute:** Add fonts only when the current set cannot differentiate information types. Each new font must serve a clear communicative purpose. Limit to 2-3 fonts maximum.

## Layout Instructions

### "Add a sidebar"

**Perceptual intent:** The primary content area is trying to serve two purposes: main content and supplementary information. These need to be visually separated so the reader can distinguish primary from secondary.

**How to execute:** Create a clear visual distinction between primary and secondary content. This may be a sidebar, but it could also be callout boxes, indented sections, or visual separators. The goal is visual separation, not specifically a sidebar.

### "Put this in a table"

**Perceptual intent:** The information has a structured relationship that would be clearer in a tabular format. Rows and columns would make comparisons easier and patterns visible.

**How to execute:** Evaluate whether a table is the right structure. If the data has clear row/column relationships, use a table. If the data is sequential, a list may be better. If the data is hierarchical, a nested structure may be better.

### "Add a box around it"

**Perceptual intent:** This content needs to be visually separated from its surroundings. It is a distinct group that should be perceived as separate.

**How to execute:** Use proximity, background color, border, or shadow to create visual separation. A box is one option, but whitespace alone may achieve the same effect with less visual weight.

## Color Instructions

### "Make it pop"

**Perceptual intent:** This element needs more emphasis. The current color does not provide sufficient contrast against its surroundings to draw attention.

**How to execute:** Increase the contrast between this element and its background or surroundings. Consider color contrast, saturation, and brightness. Ensure the result is accessible (WCAG AA contrast ratios).

### "Use our brand colors"

**Perceptual intent:** The design should feel consistent with the organization's visual identity. Color is a primary signal of identity and trust.

**How to execute:** Apply brand colors according to the brand system. Use primary brand color for emphasis, secondary for supporting elements, neutrals for body text and backgrounds. Do not force brand colors where they reduce readability.

### "Add some color"

**Perceptual intent:** The design is visually monotonous. The reader's eye has no color landmarks to aid scanning. The visual experience is flat.

**How to execute:** Add color strategically: one accent color for emphasis, used consistently. Color should serve hierarchy and scanning, not decoration.

## Content Instructions

### "Make it scannable"

**Perceptual intent:** The reader will scan, not read. The content needs scan anchors (headings, labels, visual markers, bullet points) so the reader can find what they need without reading everything.

**How to execute:** Add clear headings. Use bullet points for lists. Bold key terms. Create visual hierarchy that supports scanning. Reduce paragraph density.

### "Simplify this"

**Perceptual intent:** The cognitive load is too high. The reader is overwhelmed. The information needs to be presented in a way that manages complexity.

**How to execute:** Identify the core message. Remove non-essential information. Use progressive disclosure to layer complexity. Break dense content into chunks. Use visual hierarchy to guide the reader through the information in order of importance.

### "Make it more professional"

**Perceptual intent:** The design does not communicate the appropriate level of authority, trustworthiness, or quality for the context. The visual signals are not matching the content's intent.

**How to execute:** Increase visual consistency. Use restrained typography. Ensure precise alignment. Use a professional color palette (muted, not garish). Reduce decorative elements. Increase white space.

---

## The Translation Pattern

Every formatting instruction has a perceptual intent:

1. **Hear the instruction** ("Make the headings bigger")
2. **Identify the intent** (The hierarchy is insufficient; the reader cannot scan)
3. **Evaluate the perceptual problem** (Headings do not stand out from body text)
4. **Choose the right solution** (Increase heading-body contrast through size, weight, spacing, or color)
5. **Execute the perceptual goal** (Not just bigger headings, but clearer hierarchy)

The instruction tells you what to produce. The intent tells you why. The perceptual principle tells you how.
